The meaning of healthy cities — information sources

Every indicator in the Kanaleneiland analysis traces back to a specific WHO criterion, peer-reviewed study, or official dataset. This page documents each source, what it proved, and why it creates a specific connection in the relationship map.

The WHO 6P framework — core definition

WHO European Healthy Cities Network — Phase VII (2019–2024)

The foundational source for the entire map. The WHO defines a Healthy City as one that continuously creates and improves its physical and social environments and expands community resources — enabling people to reach their full potential. Phase VII added two non-negotiable requirements: (1) intersectoral governance — health must be embedded in all city policies; and (2) community participation — residents must be involved in decisions that affect their health.

This is why Governance sits as an enabling condition connecting to every other pillar — not as one factor among equals. Without it, all other interventions work at half capacity.
WHO European Healthy Cities Network, Phase VII 2019–2024 · who.int/europe/healthy-cities

Prosperity — economic equity

Marmot Review — Fair Society, Healthy Lives (2010)

Sir Michael Marmot introduced the concept of "causes of the causes" — proving that poverty and economic insecurity causally produce poor health outcomes through multiple simultaneous pathways. The review established that health inequalities are systematically produced by social structure, not individual choices.

Why it matters for Kanaleneiland: The 15-point socio-economic index gap is the upstream driver of the loneliness gap, mental health gap, physical impairment gap, and park access deficit simultaneously. This is the most connected bubble in the map because of Marmot's evidence.
Marmot M et al. (2010). Fair Society, Healthy Lives: The Marmot Review. UCL Institute of Health Equity, London.

WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health (CSDH, 2008)

Established that 80% of health outcomes are determined by social, economic, and environmental factors — not clinical healthcare. This is why Healthcare Access in the map connects through economic equity and housing rather than standing alone as the primary solution.

WHO CSDH (2008). Closing the gap in a generation: health equity through action on the social determinants of health. Geneva: WHO.

UNICEF Innocenti — Child Poverty in Rich Countries

Kanaleneiland's child poverty rate of 33.6% is more than double Utrecht's 13.2%. UNICEF Innocenti documents how child poverty constrains nutrition, play, education, and health service access simultaneously — creating intergenerational disadvantage. Core target of SDG 1 and SDG 3.

UNICEF Innocenti Research Centre. Child Poverty in Rich Countries report series. CBS StatLine Netherlands — Armoede en Sociale uitsluiting.

People — health & wellbeing

Holt-Lunstad et al. (2015) — PLOS Medicine

Meta-analysis of 148 studies covering over 300,000 participants. Found that social isolation and loneliness increase mortality risk by 26–32% — equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. Loneliness ranked above obesity, physical inactivity, and air pollution in its effect on mortality.

Why it matters: With a loneliness index 12 points below Utrecht — the second-largest gap in the entire dataset — Kanaleneiland carries a significant hidden mortality burden that standard physical health metrics would not capture.
Holt-Lunstad J, Smith TB, Baker M, Harris T, Stephenson D (2015). Loneliness and Social Isolation as Risk Factors for Mortality: A Meta-Analytic Review. PLOS Medicine. doi:10.1371/journal.pmed.1001867

BRE/Savills Healthy Cities Index (2023)

Provides the composite scoring framework for the Physical Health Index (59.8 Kanaleneiland vs 73 Utrecht). The 52% physical impairment rate versus 28% Utrecht — nearly double — is the most striking individual statistic in the dataset and drives the size of the Physical Health bubble.

BRE/Savills Healthy Cities Index 2023. CBS OpenData — Neighbourhood Health Survey, Table 86270ENG. opendata.cbs.nl

Healthy Cities Commission Report (2022) / NHS England

Documents that GP proximity and hospital distance are necessary conditions for health equity but not sufficient. NHS England evidence shows 80% of health outcomes are driven by social determinants rather than clinical encounters. Services must be embedded in healthy environments.

The Healthy Cities Commission (2022). Making Our Cities Healthier. NHS England — Social determinants drive 80% of health outcomes.

Place — housing, transport & built environment

WHO Housing and Health Guidelines (2018)

WHO formally classified cold, damp, overcrowded, and insecure housing as health hazards with direct causal links to respiratory disease, cardiovascular outcomes, childhood development, and mental health. Housing connects bidirectionally in the map: poverty causes poor housing, and poor housing causes disease and stress.

WHO (2018). WHO Housing and Health Guidelines. Geneva: World Health Organization. ISBN 978-92-4-155037-6.

Buildings & Cities Journal (2022) — Housing retrofit co-benefits

Documents that housing energy retrofit delivers simultaneous health and climate benefits — warmer homes reduce respiratory illness and fuel poverty while also cutting carbon emissions. This is the scientific basis for Housing connecting to Climate Resilience in the map: one investment, two returns.

Buildings and Cities Journal (2022). Retrofit co-benefits: health, wellbeing and carbon outcomes from housing improvement. UCL Open Publishing.

WHO Global Action Plan on Physical Activity (GAPPA, 2018–2030)

Establishes that active travel — walking and cycling — is one of the most cost-effective NCD prevention interventions available to cities. Physical inactivity is the fourth leading global mortality risk factor. The train station at 2.6km from Kanaleneiland's centroid directly affects whether residents can incorporate active travel into daily routines.

WHO (2018). Global Action Plan on Physical Activity 2018–2030: More Active People for a Healthier World. Geneva: WHO. ISBN 978-92-4-151418-7.

Nature Medicine (2025) — Urban transport and health equity

Confirmed mental health benefits of active mobility and documented how transport equity gaps disproportionately harm lower-income residents. Reinforces the connection between Transport & Mobility, Loneliness, and Economic Equity in the map.

Nature Medicine (2025). Urban transport equity and mental health outcomes in low-income city districts. Nature Portfolio.

Planet — green space, biodiversity & climate

PMC Systematic Review — Green Space and Health (2021)

The most important source for the Green Space bubble (27.5 points below Utrecht). Confirmed three distinct simultaneous health pathways:

1. Harm reduction — vegetation filters PM2.5, ozone, and NO2, directly reducing respiratory and cardiovascular disease.
2. Mental restoration — natural environments reduce cortisol, blood pressure, and cognitive fatigue (attention restoration effect).
3. Capacity building — accessible green space enables physical activity, social interaction, and community cohesion.

Why it matters: This three-pathway evidence is why Green Space connects to Mental Wellbeing, Physical Health, Air Quality, and Social Cohesion simultaneously — more connections than any other built-environment factor.
PMC Systematic Review (2021). Urban green space and health: A systematic review. PMID 34470696. International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health.

King's College London — Urban Mind Study (2021)

Used real-time mobile experience sampling across European cities to show that mental wellbeing scores are significantly higher in biodiverse green environments compared to uniform green spaces. Species richness amplifies the mental restoration effect. Scientific basis for Kanaleneiland's +9.3 biodiversity advantage being identified as an underused health asset.

Urban Mind Study (2021). King's College London Institute of Psychiatry, Psychology and Neuroscience. Proceedings of the Royal Society B.

WHO Global Air Quality Guidelines (2021)

WHO estimates 7 million premature deaths per year globally are attributable to air pollution. The 2021 guidelines significantly tightened PM2.5, NO2, and ozone limits. Urban trees and vegetation are the primary nature-based solution for localised air pollution — the basis for the Air Quality bubble depending on Green Space in the map.

WHO (2021). WHO Global Air Quality Guidelines: PM2.5, PM10, Ozone, NO2, SO2 and CO. Geneva: WHO.

IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6) — Urban chapter

Documents that low-income urban districts experience disproportionate exposure to urban heat islands, flooding, and extreme weather. They have less tree cover, older housing, and fewer adaptation resources. Climate resilience is an equity issue — which is why Climate Resilience connects to both Biodiversity and Housing in the map.

IPCC (2021). Sixth Assessment Report, Chapter 6: Cities, Settlements and Key Infrastructure. Working Group II.

Participation — social cohesion & resilience

PMC — Social cohesion and mental health (multiple systematic reviews)

Confirmed that social cohesion and mental health are mutually reinforcing — each strengthens the other. Communities with stronger social bonds show lower rates of depression and anxiety even when controlling for income and physical health. This bidirectional relationship is why Social Cohesion both receives from and sends to multiple other nodes in the map.

PMC Systematic Reviews on social cohesion and mental health in urban populations. PubMed Central, multiple publications 2018–2023.

WHO Phase VII Participation Requirements (2019–2024)

Made community participation in governance decisions a mandatory criterion for Healthy City designation — not optional. Residents, particularly from disadvantaged communities, must participate in decisions about housing, transport, green space, and health services. Basis for Social Cohesion connecting to Governance in the map.

WHO European Healthy Cities Network (2019). Phase VII 2019–2024: Goals and Requirements. Copenhagen: WHO Regional Office for Europe.

Peace — governance & equity

WHO Health in All Policies (HiAP) Framework (2013)

Requires city governments to systematically assess health implications of decisions across all sectors — housing, transport, economic development, education, and planning. Without HiAP governance, the green space team, housing team, transport team, and health team each work in isolation. Kanaleneiland's biodiversity strength cannot address its mental health deficit unless governance explicitly links the two.

WHO Regional Office for Europe (2013). Health in All Policies. Helsinki Statement. Eighth Global Conference on Health Promotion.

Empirical data — CBS OpenData Netherlands

CBS Kerncijfers Wijken en Buurten — Table 86270ENG

All numerical scores in the map — the Kanaleneiland values and Utrecht benchmark figures — originate from Statistics Netherlands' neighbourhood-level health and wellbeing dataset. Published annually for all 2,500+ Dutch neighbourhoods. Covers impairment rates by type, loneliness scores, perceived health, child poverty, green space accessibility, biodiversity, and facility distances.

CBS (2023). Kerncijfers wijken en buurten. Table 86270ENG. opendata.cbs.nl/#/CBS/en/dataset/86270ENG/table

Gezondheidsmonitor Volwassenen en Ouderen (RIVM/GGD/CBS)

The survey behind the raw health indicators. Run every four years by all 25 Dutch regional health services (GGDs), RIVM, and CBS. The 2022 edition surveyed 364,557 people. Uses small-area estimation (kleine-domeinschattingen) modelling to produce reliable neighbourhood-level figures for districts the size of Kanaleneiland.

RIVM/GGD/CBS Gezondheidsmonitor Volwassenen en Ouderen 2020/2022. rivm.nl/gezondheidsmonitors/volwassenen-en-ouderen · monitorgezondheid.nl

Three leverage points — what the sources together recommend

1. Economic equity — the upstream cause

Addressing income inequality and child poverty simultaneously improves physical health, mental health, loneliness, and social resilience. The Marmot "causes of causes" principle: fix the root and all downstream gaps begin to close. (Source: Marmot Review 2010 · CSDH 2008 · UNICEF Innocenti)

2. Green space access — 4 returns from 1 investment

Closing the 27.5-point park access deficit delivers harm reduction, mental restoration, physical activity enablement, and social cohesion through three confirmed simultaneous pathways. Beneluxlaan as a green corridor leverages the existing biodiversity asset. (Source: PMC 2021 · King's College 2021 · WHO AQG 2021)

3. Intersectoral governance — the enabling condition

Without Health in All Policies governance, the other five pillars cannot connect. Kanaleneiland's biodiversity strength, for example, cannot address its mental health deficit unless governance explicitly links the two. (Source: WHO HiAP 2013 · WHO Phase VII 2019–2024)
